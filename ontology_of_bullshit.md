## An Ontology of Bullshit

Once, when driving across Australia from West to East, I decided to read [a collection of Aesop's fables](https://www.amazon.com/gp/product/B00DMCV46M/ref=kinw_myk_ro_title). I was familiar with many stories from the collection, relayed to me in one way or another through my religious childhood, but I had never tried to read through a collection from cover to cover. What I found was not so much a collection of ancient wisdom, but a series of stories that sought to justify a world view I didn't agree with. This view was no doubt 'updated' by Victorian translators and modern editors, but it still struck me as being from a different way of life.

Since reading that collection, I've wondered what readers 2000 years in the future will think of our time, and what our stories relaying 'Universal Truths' will say about us. Since allegories in the traditional style have somewhat gone out of fashion, I've turned my attention to their internet age equivalents, Bullshit Facebook stories.

### The Repairman
The story goes that Henry Ford (or a Factory Owner or a Car Owner..) sees Nikola Tesla (or Thomas Edison or A Mechanic, or an old handyman...) about a malfunctioning piece of equipment (or a car, or a boat, or a plumbing issue, or a....), the expert then immediately marks a spot with chalk (or .....), fixing the machine. They then send an exorbitant bill. Upon being asked for an itemised report, they send a pithy response:
1. Marking the spot: $2
2. Knowing where: $998

The usual closing summary is that the expert is paid for their lifetime of experience, even if they do not appear to do much in the moment. While this can be true, I think a deeper reading of the text could reveal some more interesting conclusions.
